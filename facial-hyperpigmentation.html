Decoding Dark Spots – 5 Reasons for Facial Hyperpigmentation

Take a good, long look in the mirror. Noticing some darker areas of skin around your face? This common skin issue is called hyperpigmentation and is caused by increased production of pigment in the skin. There are lots of different forms of pigment, but the most common is melanin which is produced by melanocytes just beneath the most superficial layer, called your epidermis. There are different mechanisms that can cause hyperpigmentation, such as increased number of melanocytes or increased production of melanin. In any case, there’s really no need for alarm as most instances of facial hyperpigmentation are anything but life threatening. Nonetheless, it’s important to make sure you’re not dealing with any underlying issues that could be pointing to more serious health threats. Wondering whether you need to see a doctor or just buy a new bottle of lotion? Understanding these 5 reasons for facial hyperpigmentation should guide your next steps.

5 Reasons for Facial Hyperpigmentation

1. Post Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation

Inflammation can occur as the result of acne, allergies, and skin conditions like eczema. When inflammatory cells react to these skin issues, melanin is pushed deeper into the layers of the skin and is consequently trapped. So when inflammation subsides, you’re left with dark spots that may take quite some time to fade.

Because inflammation first has to subside before the dark spots can be seen, there really is no way to tell if the resulting marks are a result of the inflammation. The only real indication you might be able to use is the distribution of the spots. For instance, dark spots from acne can be fainter, appearing over areas of previous pimples and blemishes. On the other hand, dark spots that result from eczema can develop in larger patches.

Treatments for post inflammatory hyperpigmentation can be limited because the melanin becomes trapped deep within the skin. The best way to prevent exacerbation would be to avoid another episode of inflammation by using topical steroids and anti-inflammatory formulations. It can also help to steer clear of prolonged exposure to sunlight for a while.

2. Melasma

Occurring more commonly in women, melasma is a condition that often develops as the result of an increase in estrogen. That’s why it’s usually experienced by pregnant women or those taking contraceptive pills. Women with relatives who have melasma are also much more likely to develop the condition themselves. There’s also a strong relationship between melasma and sunlight exposure, including both ultraviolet and intense visible light.

Preventing and treating melasma starts with avoiding the factors that could cause it. For instance, women who develop the hyperpigmentation as a result of the use of contraceptive pills should cease intake to normalize hormonal balance. To protect skin from ultraviolet and intense visible light, slathering on sunscreen with at least 50 SPF before stepping out into the sun is vital.

Finally, if you want to speed up the reduction of pigmentation, you can use a variety of topical treatments. Usually, the most effective creams and lotions for melasma incorporate hydroquinone. Before you go out to look for an over the counter solution though, it pays to keep in mind that some of these bleaching agents can cause over-bleaching. In some people, they may even cause allergic reactions leading to inflammation that can develop secondary hyperpigmentation.

Always consult a dermatologist before trying out any treatment for your skin to make sure you don’t exacerbate the problem.

3. Freckles

For some people, freckles are a pretty skin pigmentation that adds a touch of character. These small flecks of light brown pigment can be scattered over the face, shoulders, and other areas of the body, and occur more commonly in fairer skinned individuals.

If you don’t like the way they look, you can prevent the development of freckles by protecting your skin from overexposure to sunlight. For those who really want to go the extra mile, visiting a dermatologist to get pigment laser treatments is an effective way to zap away the small flecks of pigmentation.

In some rare cases, harmless freckles can develop into lentigo maligna – a precancerous condition that risks the development of full blown malignant melanoma. If you suspect that your freckles are starting to pose a risk to your health, be sure to seek prompt medical attention and treatment.

4. Seborrhoeic Keratosis Dermatosis Papulosis Nigra

Benign and harmless, seborrhoeic keratosis dermatosis papulosis nigra is a skin condition that causes the development of dark skin growths. They can occur to anyone, but are usually more pronounced in individuals with darker skin tones. While they are completely harmless, most of those who suffer the condition have them removed for cosmetic concerns. Dermatologists can scrape them off under local anaesthetic or can remove them with electrocautery or laser ablation. Keep in mind though that the growths can return after some time, and the treatments may lead to the development of post inflammatory hyperpigmentation.

5. Rare Causes

Aside from the previously mentioned causes, there are a few less common, more dangerous reasons for the development of hyperpigmentation. Lichen planus pigmentosus for example, causes the appearance of large, flat dark spots that are particularly pronounced, especially in individuals with dark skin tones. Lupus, an autoimmune disease characterized by skin inflammation, is also known to cause hyperpigmentation of the face. Types of lupus known to cause dark spots on the face include discoid lupus and subacute lupus, which can also lead to significant scarring. Finally, ochronosis is another rare cause of hyperpigmentation that occurs as the result of overexposure to skin lightening agents like hydroquinone. When you use too much whitening product, the active ingredient accumulates under the surface of the skin, leading to the development of dark spots. The sad truth is that some people don’t know they’re exposing themselves to whitening agents, and thus unwittingly cause the hyperpigmentation themselves. There are very few treatments for ochronosis and doctors are still trying to come up with effective remedies.

While these 5 reasons are the most common causes for hyperpigmentation, self-diagnosis isn’t something any of us should practice. Use this information as a guide to help you better understand what’s happening under your skin, but be sure to visit a health care professional to get a solid diagnosis and safe, appropriate treatment. Remember – your skin is your first barrier against the elements around you. Make sure to protect what protects you – keep your skin healthy by visiting a dermatologist regularly and steering clear of factors that could lead to problems down the line.
