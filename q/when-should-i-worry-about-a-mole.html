When should I worry about a mole?

· ·

I have got a lot of moles, how do I know which ones I should worry about?

Most of us have at least a few moles (referred to medically as melanocytic naevi). Moles themselves are entirely harmless and represent an overgrowth of normal pigment producing cells (melanocytes) that are located in the skin. Rarely, these pigment producing cells can become cancerous leading to the development of melanoma, a form of skin cancer that has the potential to spread elsewhere in the body.

Whilst rare overall, melanoma can affect individuals of any age and is one of the most common forms of cancer affecting young adults. Importantly, melanoma is potentially more amenable to treatment if caught at an early stage. In view of this how can we identify concerning moles?

There are several features that can enable the identification of dangerous moles:-

  • An irregular, assymetrical appearance
  • Significant change in the appearance of an existing mole or the development of a new mole
  • A mole differing significantly in appearance to other moles the 'ugly duckling' sign

Appearance of the mole

Generally moles that are harmless will have a symmetrical, uniform appearance. Conversely dangerous moles have an irregular appearance. The ABCD rules provide an aid to the identification of a concerning moles.

A: Assymetry

Benign (harmless) moles usually are symmetrical in their appearance with one half of the mole appearing as a mirror image of the other half. Concerning moles are more frequently assymetrical.

B: Border irregularity

Benign moles generally are characterized by a smooth, regular border. Concerning moles are more likely to have an irregular border.

C: Color variation

Moles can exhibit a number of different colors including light brown, dark brown, black and red. Benign moles more commonly have a single color throughout, in contrast, concerning moles may have several different colors.

D: Diameter

Benign moles are more likely to be smaller in size, whereas dangerous moles can grow to a significantly larger size. 0.5cm is sometimes quoted as a threshold above which moles are more concerning, however this size cutoff is somewhat arbitrary.

The 'Ugly Duckling' sign

For many patients, the moles present at different sites on the body have similar appearances. The presence of a mole that looks significantly different from other moles on the body is known as the 'ugly duckling' sign and can alert to the presence of a concerning mole even where that mole does not appear overtly concerning in isolation.

Change in a mole

Approximately half of melanomas are thought to arise as completely new skin lesions whereas the other half are thought to arise from existing moles that have changed in their appearance.

It is normal to develop new moles in teenage years and into the early twenties. At older ages, particulary after the early 30s, the development of a new mole is potentially more concerning and usually should be examined by a dermatologist.

Moles do change in appearance over the lifetime, however a significant change in the appearance of an existing mole can be a sign of skin cancer. In practise is is difficult for a patient to distinguish the normal evolution of a mole from a concerning change and a significant change in the size, shape or pigmentary pattern requires review by a dermatologist.

Other risk factors

A number of factors increase the risk of developing a melanoma:

  • If you have previously been treated for a melanoma
  • If a family member, particularly first degree relatives have previously been treated for melanoma
  • If you have a large number of irregular moles
  • If you have had extensive sun light exposure, particularly episodes of blistering sunburn
  • Where you are immunosupressed particularly as the consequence

For patients falling into these groups, closer surveillance with regular mole checks may be advised.

When should you see a dermatologist?

If you have any concerns about a mole then it should be checked by a dermatologist who can also perform a check of the rest of your skin. Although the above rules are a guide to which moles are worrying, it is possible for moles to meet some of these criteria and to be harmless, similarly concerning moles do not have to exhibit all of these features.

A dermatologist has years of experience in evaluating skin lesions, in addition they can examine the pattern of skin lesions in closer detail using a dermatoscope. If a mole is concerning then removal is recommended in order that the cellular pattern of the mole can be examined with a microscope. In some cases, where a mole has some conerning features but is not thought to be a skin cancer or for patients with lots of concerning moles, then a period of monitoring may be advised. Mole mapping photographs may be taken of the entire skin with close up dermoscopic photos of specific moles of interest.
