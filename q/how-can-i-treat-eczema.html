How can I treat eczema?

SkinAlpha Team · ·

What is the best treatment for eczema?

Eczema (atopic dermatitis) causes dry, red and flaky skin. It results from a combination of impaired barrier function, which allows environmental allergens to enter the skin, and overactivity of the immune system within the skin. Treatments are targeted at both of these aspects.

Topical treatments (creams and ointments):

  • Emollients (moisturisers) such as cetraben, doublebase and epaderm ointment restore the skin barrier function and should be used multiple times per day
  • Soap substitutes such as oilatum or dermol 500 are used in the place of soap or shower gel which contain harsh detergents and can damage the barrier function of the skin
  • Mild topical steroids (e.g. eumovate) can be used to mild areas of involvement, for children or for the face in adults
  • Moderate / strong topical steroids (such as elocon, dermovate) can be used to more extensive areas of involvement on the body
  • Non steroid topical therapies such as Protopic (topical tacrolimus) are of particular benefit for use on the face as they do not have some of the side effects associated with long term usage of topical steroids

Generally the use of topical steroids as directed by a dermatologist is safe. There is a theoretical risk of thinning of the skin where strong topical steroids are used for long periods of time to the same body site. This gives rise to an appearance similar to stretch marks which although unsightly are not dangerous. In practice this is very rare and usually is seen only where strong topical steroids are used excessively to the same site for many weeks or months. Another potential side effect of topical steroids, particularly on the face is the development of secondary rashes including acne, rosacea and peri-oral dermatitis.

Phototherapy (Light therapy)

Narrow band UVB (TL01) phototherapy is used most commonly. Generally this is administered in hospital 2-3 times per week for several months. It can be an effective therapy for eczema, particuarly where involvement is too widespread for topical treatments to be practical. Side effects are similar to those of sunlight exposure and include the development of photosensitive rashes, sunburn-like reactions, a very small increase in the risk of skin cancer and photoaging.

Oral corticosteroids

The most commonly used oral corticosteroid is prednisolone. This is an effective treatment for short term flares of eczema providing rapid relieve, however generally eczema will flare on discontinuing corticosteroids and long term usage is not advised due to side effects which include weightgain, high blood pressure, osteoporosis and diabetes.

Systemic therapies

Systemic therapies aim to suppress the excessive immune activity that is driving immune activity in the skin. These can prove very effective therapies for eczema however they do have the potential for side effects and require close monitoring which generally includes blood tests weekly for the first few weeks reducing in frequency to once every 3 months when stable.

The most commonly used systemic agents are as follows:

  • Methotrexate: Potential side effects include irritation of the liver (hepatitis). As a consequence it is not advised to drink significant quantities of alcohol whilst taking methotrexate. Another potentially serious side effect is immunosupression - rarely methotrexate can cause a large drop in white blood cell levels that leads to a risk of dangerous infections.
  • Azathioprine: Potential side effects include immunosupression and a small increase in the risk of cancers, particularly skin cancers with prolonged usage.
  • Mycophenolate mofetil: The main risk is immunosupression requiring regular blood monitoring.
  • Ciclosporin: In addition to immunosupression and a small increase in the risk of malignancy, ciclosporin causes a stress on the kidneys. If used long term (for years) this can lead to kidney failure and therefore the usage of ciclosporin is generally restricted to a maximum of 1 year for skin problems.
