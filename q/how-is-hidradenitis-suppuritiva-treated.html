How is hidradenitis suppuritiva treated?

SkinAlpha Team · ·

Can hidradenitis suppuritiva be cured?

Hidradenitis suppuritiva (HS) is characterized by the development of multiple boils and abscesses affecting the groin, armpits and sometimes other areas including under the breasts. Early stages can generally be treated effectively, however where it progresses to a severe stage it is more challening to effectively treat. Treatments include the following:

  • Antibacterial washes, such as hibiscrub
  • Topical antibiotics that are applied to new areas of inflammation e.g. topical clindamycin (Dalacin T)
  • Extended courses of oral antibiotics, particularly tetracycline derivatives (e.g. 3 month course of oral lymecycline)
  • Where the above measures are not effective, a more powerful combination of antibiotics - rifampicin and clindamycin - can be prescribed, usually for a 3 month period

When the above treatments are not effective or not tolerated other options include the following:-

  • Acitretin can be of benefit to some patients, however is contraindicated in females of childbearing age as it causes birth malformations and can persist in the body for up to 3 years
  • Isotretinoin can be of benefit for patients with coexistent acne or acneiform lesions however does not tend to be very effective for HS in general
  • Dapsone is another therapy that can be tried. It is of benefit for some patients however requires close blood monitoring.
  • Intravenous antibiotics: Patients with severe HS can significantly experience a significant improvement in symptoms with a 1-2 week course of intravenous antibiotics. Generally this requires admission to hospital. Unfortunately there is not clear evidence that this improves the course of the disease and it can return to baseline when intravenous antibioitics are discontinued.
  • Anti-TNF therapy such as adalimumab is generally reserved for patients who have failed other standard treatment options. It can benefit some patients however the benefit is not always sustained.
