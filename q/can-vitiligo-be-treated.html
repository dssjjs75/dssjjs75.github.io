Can vitiligo be treated?

SkinAlpha Team · ·

I have developed white patches on the face and hands, my doctor has diagnosed vitiligo - should I see a dermatologist? Is there any treatment that works?

Vitiligo is a challenging condition and the response to treatment can vary from very good to dissapointing. Usually topical (cream) therapies are tried in the first instance. A typical regimen might consist of a topical steroid for 6 weeks followed by topical tacrolimus (Protopic) for a minumum of several months. Response can differ according to body site with the face often improving more than some other sites such as the back of the hands. If topical therapies are ineffective or the areas of depigmentation are too extensive, another option is narrow band UVB therapy (TL01). This requires attending the hospital 2-3 times per week for approximately 3 months.

For localised vitiligo that is resistent to other treatment modalities pigment producing cells (melanocytes) can be transplanted from another site on the body. This is generally more effective for a specific subtype 'segmental' vitiligo. Rarely the depigmentation resulting from vitiligo is so extensive that only small patches of normally pigmented skin remain. In this case bleaching of the remaining areas may lead to an overall improvement in the appearance, however this is a permanent treatment so should be considered carefully.

Whilst standard treatments are in progress or if treatments fail to restore pigmentation then camouflage makeup can effectively cover areas of abnormal pigmentation. Organisations such as Changing Faces offer a camouflage makeup consultation service. In addition commercially available products such as Dermablend permit the patient to obtain a good colour match.
